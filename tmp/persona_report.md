Persona Name & Role: Raj Patel, Emergency Medicine Consultant (Clinical Lead) at a busy urban A&E. Leads the clinical team and oversees care delivery in the Emergency Department (ED). Reports to the Medical Director; manages junior doctors and works closely with the ED Matron. 20 years in Emergency Medicine, respected for championing patient safety and flow. British Indian, multilingual (speaks some Gujarati) – personally attuned to language needs from treating diverse patients in London.
Brief Personal Background: Age 50. Became ED Clinical Lead 3 years ago. Has seen the department struggle with communication barriers as the local population includes many non-English speakers. Remembers a critical incident where a patient’s allergy wasn’t conveyed due to a language gap, which deeply affected Raj’s commitment to improvement. Passionate about equity in care due to personal roots in a bilingual household.
Goals & Outcomes:
•	Primary Objectives: Ensure every patient receives safe, timely treatment regardless of language   . Aims to hit the 4-hour ED target while maintaining quality – Raj coordinates clinical staff to avoid delays in care . Wants to reduce diagnostic errors and adverse events related to miscommunication.
•	KPIs Tracked: Department 4-hour wait performance and “left without being seen” rates (as proxy for patient experience). Monitors clinical incident reports involving communication issues and aims for zero serious incidents from language barriers   . Tracks patient satisfaction scores, especially for patients with limited English.
•	Success Metrics: Fewer complaints or safety reports citing interpreter delays. 99% of critical patients have documented interpreter support or translation when needed. Improved outcomes: e.g. reduced time to treatment in triage for non-English speakers. If the new system can cut communication-related delays by even 5-10 minutes per patient, Raj sees that as success.
•	Desired Outcomes from System: In Raj’s words: “I want to focus on treating illness, not scrambling to find interpreters. If this platform lets me instantly understand my patient’s symptoms in their language, we can make faster and safer decisions”. He hopes it will ensure accurate history-taking and consent in critical cases, and avoid the “linguistic blind spots” that have led to omissions in the past . Raj expects the system will also speed up discharges by providing translated instructions so patients truly comprehend their treatment and follow-ups.
Wants, Needs & Expectations:
•	Daily Workflow Needs: During a shift, Raj often does “consultant in charge” rounds, where he must review many patients. He needs instant access to interpretation at triage and during resuscitations. It must be fast – flipping open a tablet or triggering an interpreter mode without leaving the bedside. Clinical accuracy is paramount: he needs medical terms (e.g. drug names, symptoms) correctly translated. Raj relies on clear documentation; he expects any translated conversation to be noted (e.g. as a transcript in the EPR) for medico-legal safety .
•	Information & Resources: Raj stays updated on guidance from NHS England and the GMC about communication. He knows GMC guidance requires all efforts for effective patient communication . He has hospital policy binders on using interpreters, and he frequently consults the Patient Experience team data on language needs. He expects the system’s output (like translated discharge summaries) to integrate into the patient’s record for continuity of care.
•	Technology Expectations: Tech-savvy enough (uses a smartphone for clinical calculators) but no time for fiddling. Wants a reliable, one-click solution – e.g. an app that auto-detects language and starts translating speech in real time. It must handle multiple languages and even dialect nuances. He expects utter fidelity: clinical nuance must be preserved (for instance, differentiating “chest tightness” vs “chest pain” correctly). He’s open-minded to AI but insists on a human-quality standard – ≥99% accuracy as promised . Raj prefers a system with a simple interface that any doctor can use under pressure (maybe voice-activated). He also expects cultural competency warnings – e.g. if he says something that might be culturally insensitive, the system should flag or adapt it.
•	Support & Training Needs: Given Raj’s seniority, he’s championing this project, so he underwent a demo and training. He needed to understand how to verify the translation accuracy. He expects ongoing support – a contact if the device fails during a resuscitation at 2 AM. He also seeks training for his team: he has arranged sessions for junior docs on how to use the device in practice, and he personally mentors them on the importance of checking understanding (e.g. asking patients to repeat info back). He would appreciate quick-reference guides (laminated cards or built-in tutorial) for edge cases (like using text-to-speech for deaf or literacy issues).

He communicates with his team in daily huddles – he has already incorporated the translation service into their morning briefing (“We have this tool; use it whenever needed, don’t hesitate”). With patients, he is empathetic; he’ll still maintain eye contact and speak to the patient (not the device) while using the interpreter – a practice he values from training . Biggest Pain Points & Unmet Needs:
•	Frustrations/Challenges: Raj’s current frustrations revolve around the slow and fragmented interpreter process. In critical cases, waiting even 15 minutes for a telephone interpreter is agonizing. He recalls nights where a patient was in agony and the team wasted time finding someone who spoke their language. It feels “like practicing blindfolded.” He’s also experienced inaccurate ad-hoc interpretation – e.g. a well-meaning relative omitting details; this lack of fidelity haunts him . Another pain: consent conversations for emergency surgery are extremely hard through a phone interpreter on speaker; he worries something gets lost or the patient isn’t truly understanding the risks.
•	Workflow Bottlenecks: Triage is a major bottleneck – if the nurse can’t get basic info due to language, everything backs up. Raj often steps in to prioritize those patients, but still, valuable time is lost. Another bottleneck: rounding on admitted patients from overnight – often their initial history is incomplete due to no interpreter at 3 AM, so Raj must redo the history next morning with proper support, costing time. This duplication could be avoided.
•	System Limitations: Telephone interpreting is inconsistent and costly. Some interpreters are excellent, but others have poor audio or lack medical vocabulary, and it’s hard to gauge quality. Also, cultural nuance is missing – he has had interpreters translate literally but not convey the patient’s tone or hesitation. Current translation of documents is practically non-existent in real time; giving patients English printouts when they can’t read them is futile. Raj feels the hospital’s approach has been reactive, not proactive – e.g., relying on family to interpret in off-hours, which he knows is “strongly discouraged” in policy . This gaps leave him uneasy about compliance and ethics.
•	Tasks to Automate: If Raj could wave a wand, he’d automate instant consent form translation – currently he sits with patients going line by line with an interpreter; an on-demand translated consent form (with easy-read explanations) would save him time and ensure the patient can read it later. Also, automating the triage questionnaire in multiple languages – e.g., a tablet that asks symptom questions in the patient’s language as they check in – would remove burden from nurses. Raj also wishes the system could auto-flag any untranslated content in discharge notes (like a medicine name) and provide an explanation in patient’s language.
•	Gaps in Current Solutions: The largest gap: lack of real-time availability. Emergencies can’t wait. Also, current systems don’t integrate – phone interpreters don’t document in the record; written translations take days through the trust’s admin, thus useless at discharge. Cultural competency is another gap; nothing currently checks if phrasing might offend or confuse (Raj has seen health materials inadvertently use idioms that don’t translate culturally). Patients with low literacy are not addressed at all by current means – giving a dense leaflet doesn’t ensure comprehension. These unmet needs directly impact Raj’s work – he sometimes spends extra time rewriting discharge instructions in simpler English or using Google Translate to print something (which he knows isn’t officially approved and might be error-prone ). This is not sustainable.

•	Preferred Communication Methods (On the Job): Ben, being a nurse, communicates with many people constantly:

With language barriers, previously he’d use gestures or ask “okay?” and hope nods are genuine; now he’ll rely on translation to maintain his normal explanatory style. He likes to chat a bit to build rapport but that was nearly impossible with a language gap – maybe with the translator he can do a tiny bit of small talk in the patient’s language (like “I see you’re wearing a football jersey – do you like football?” translated – something humanizing like that, if time allows). This could endear patients and reduce their anxiety, which he values.
o	With colleagues, in the thick of ED, it’s often shorthand and direct (“I need a hand in bed 5” or “He’s hypotensive, get fluids”). For language barrier cases, he might quickly brief a colleague “He speaks only Arabic, I’m using the translator for now”. He ensures team members know the plan. If handing over a patient to another nurse or ward, he communicates any communication needs clearly: “He doesn’t speak English; we have explained everything via translator and here’s a printout in his language of the instructions – please use the device if he asks questions.” Thus, his communication ensures continuity.
o	With doctors, it’s collaborative; he might say to a doc, “I’ve got most of the history using the translator, here’s what I found” and the doc might then take the device to do their consultation. He expects that teamwork – passing the translation tool like passing the baton.
o	With family members, if present and English-speaking, he keeps them informed too. Sometimes family want to interpret themselves; Ben will tactfully encourage using the professional tool: “Thank you, we have a device to ensure nothing is missed – you can certainly add anything if needed.” He doesn’t want to sideline family but also doesn’t want them solely responsible for interpreting critical info, in line with policy . If family doesn’t speak English either, he’ll use group translation sessions, e.g., talk to patient and family together via the device’s speaker so all hear the same message in their language, to avoid confusion.
o	Documentation is another communication form – he writes nursing notes. He’ll likely note “[Language] translation used.” Possibly some direct quotes of patient if relevant, translated into English for record. He might even attach or reference a translation output if that’s part of workflow (maybe not yet, but perhaps printed instructions become part of the record).
o	He communicates feedback to management, possibly through morning briefings or directly to Lucy or Ellie if he sees something notable. He values being heard as a staff member, and since he’s a champion user, he will share both successes and problems frankly. He might say “It works great for Spanish and Polish yesterday, but struggled with that Somali dialect – we might need a workaround there.”
o	Overarchingly, his communication style is patient-centered and collegial. The translator tool doesn’t change that; it’s just a new medium he’s integrating. He remains friendly, often addressing patient directly (even if translation voice is intermediate) – he knows to always face the patient and talk as if they understand, not to the machine. This preserves a human connection.
Biggest Pain Points & Unmet Needs:
•	Current Frustrations: As a front-liner, Ben’s frustrations are very immediate. Top of the list: not understanding what a patient is trying to tell him. It’s deeply frustrating when a patient is clearly in distress or trying to convey something important (like where their pain is or how severe) and he can’t decipher it due to language. It makes him feel inadequate and the patient unsafe. There have been times he only found out about a key symptom later when an interpreter arrived or a bilingual relative came – it bothers him that the patient had to wait or possibly suffer until then. Another frustration is the inefficiency: waiting for a phone interpreter can halt an entire assessment. He might have 4 other patients to attend, but he’s stuck on hold or repeating things into a phone. This raises his stress because he’s thinking about the others too. He also dislikes depending on ad-hoc methods: he’s used Google Translate in desperation, but he’s aware it can be nonsense or even dangerous if wrong, so he feels uneasy doing it (the Telegraph backing to avoid Google in healthcare resonates with him ). But if it’s the only quick way, he’s been torn. That moral distress (choice between no info or possibly flawed info) is a pain point. Another major frustration: seeing patients confused or dissatisfied. Ben really cares about patient comfort. When he has a patient who can’t understand what’s happening – they might get anxious, start wandering, or repeatedly call for help because they’re not sure if they’ve been forgotten – it saddens and frustrates him. He ends up spending extra time trying to reassure them with gestures or fetch translators, but sometimes not as effectively as he’d like. It’s also frustrating when patients or families resort to using their children to interpret – he feels it’s inappropriate (he’s had a 10-year-old trying to explain her dad’s symptoms; he engaged the phone interpreter instead, per guidelines, but that was a bit late by the time they arrived). He also occasionally encounters cultural misunderstandings, like once a female patient from a conservative culture wouldn’t answer questions about pregnancy with male staff around. Without an interpreter to facilitate privately, it delayed care. Those episodes show him the limitation of current communication approach.
•	Workflow Bottlenecks: For Ben, in-the-moment bottlenecks often revolve around hunting for help. For example, triaging one patient could take triple time, causing a queue, because he had to arrange interpretation. That backlog can cascade: other patients wait longer, maybe get worse or angry. It’s a domino effect he’s seen. Sometimes he’s forced to multi-task poorly: like trying to communicate with a patient using a translation app on his phone while also manually checking their blood pressure – he can do it, but it splits focus. If the new system can at least speak aloud, he can focus more on clinical tasks while it speaks or listens. Another bottleneck is handover: if he doesn’t get full info due to language barrier, the doctor then has to essentially start anew when interpreter arrives – duplication of work and wasted time. Also, calls to interpreter service often involve navigating a menu, waiting – in that time he could have prepared meds or charts, but he must pay attention to the phone. If multiple staff need interpreters at once, usually only one call can happen at a time due to limited speakerphones, etc., causing a lineup internally for the phone. If one patient has multiple cycles of interpreter needs (initial history, later consent, then discharge), each requires re-calling possibly, adding overhead. The new system ideally stays with the patient throughout to avoid re-requests. Additionally, when a patient is admitted upstairs, the ward might need a summary or teaching how to continue communication – not exactly Ben’s duty, but he often finds himself briefing them. If the ward isn’t familiar or lacks the device, that continuity breaks, which is disheartening because progress made in ED could be lost. He hopes the system is hospital-wide to avoid that.
•	System Limitations (pre-implementation): The previous system (human interpreters by phone or in person) has human limitations: phone interpreters can’t see context (like patient’s facial expressions or if patient doesn’t understand something they might not say it; an in-person interpreter might notice confusion on a patient’s face and clarify, a phone one can’t). Also phone interpreters sometimes mishear things especially with medical acronyms or drug names – Ben had experiences where he had to spell out a drug letter by letter over the phone. If the patient has a strong accent even in their own language, a remote interpreter could struggle too. Another limitation: scheduling – ED is unscheduled, so no chance to pre-arrange interpreter, which is where technology can shine for immediacy. And face-to-face interpreters are rarely available at odd hours or for quick consults (they’re more for planned stuff). So the majority solution has been phone interpreters, which are impersonal and sometimes the line quality is poor, adding frustration. Also, as a male nurse, he’s aware female patients from some cultures might not be comfortable talking about certain things via a male voice telephone interpreter (or vice versa). The translation AI voice might not circumvent that completely, but if it has options for male/female voices or just being an impartial computer voice, maybe that’s less of an issue – not sure, but an interesting thought. Non-verbal cues: if a patient gestures or shows something, a human interpreter might adapt translation more fluidly than a machine. He’s curious if the AI picks up any tone (like if patient’s voice sounds worried or angry – a human interpreter might alert him, but an AI might just translate content). So some emotional subtleties could be lost; he’ll have to rely on his nursing observation to fill that gap (which he does anyway – reading body language). Also pre-new system, lack of coverage for some languages: the phone service may not have an immediate interpreter for certain dialects or rare languages at 2 AM, leaving them high and dry. He had one patient speak a rare African language where they ended up using French (which the patient sort of understood) as a bridge – not ideal. That limitation hopefully narrows with AI that can handle more languages or at least main ones.
•	Time-Consuming Workarounds: Without a good system, Ben often had to over-simplify communication which is risky – e.g., using universal gestures or one-word statements. That might save time but not truly ensure understanding (he once gestured “sleep” to a patient when meaning sedation for a procedure – the patient misunderstood and started trying to sleep etc.). So oversimplification was a side effect of lack of better tools. He’s eager to stop that and give fuller explanations through translation. Another time-consuming thing: documentation after using ad-hoc communication – if something was unclear, he writes a note “unable to ascertain X due to language barrier” which then triggers someone else to follow up. It’s double handling. He’d prefer to get info first hand and document it fully right then.
Despite frustrations, Ben has remained resourceful and understanding. He knows many colleagues have it worse (imagine being a patient who doesn’t speak the local language – that’s scarier). So he’s empathetic and patient, but very ready for a better solution.
